How it works

The dots keep switching until they settle.

Pick how many groups you want.

The computer guesses groups, finds each group middle, and moves every dot to the nearest middle again and again.

Outliers

One weird point can pull a group.

A lonely dot can drag the group middle.

Every dot must join a group, so one far-away point can pull the nearest group away from the main pile.

Look here

Investigate extreme observations before trusting the final partition. The right fix could be transformation, scaling, or a different clustering method.

You do not pick the group count yet.

One tree can show many possible group answers.

The tree keeps every join, so you can look at big groups first and smaller groups later.

Decision to make next

Decide whether you care more about nested interpretation or a single flat answer. If the tree matters, hierarchical clustering is often the better explainer.

How to read it

Closest dots join first.

The tree grows upward one join at a time.

The only number that matters on the tree is join height: higher joins mean the two parts were less alike.

3
Look here

Left and right position do not matter much. Up and down height is the important clue.

Decision to make next

If early merges look sensible and late merges jump sharply, the data likely contains a few meaningful groups rather than a smooth continuum.

What counts as close?

Same size and same shape are not the same.

Two patterns can match in different ways.

One distance looks for similar amounts. The other looks for the same up-and-down shape.

Look here

The same shape can stay together even when one line is much higher.

Decision to make next

Ask whether absolute size or relative pattern matters more in your domain. The answer should drive the distance metric before you ever draw the tree.

Try this in R 
d_euclid <- dist(x)
d_corr <- as.dist(1 - cor(t(x)))
